# Queens Lesson Flow

Use this page as a progressive teaching path from basic constraints to endgame commitment.

## Beginner: Touching Rule First

Start by teaching the most local constraint. Once players internalize touching exclusions, later eliminations feel natural.

<Canvas>
  <Story of={StrategyStories.CrownBlocksNeighbors} />
</Canvas>

### What to look for

- Every adjacent cell (including diagonals) becomes unavailable.
- This is true regardless of row, column, or region shape.
- The touching rule is often the fastest first pass.

## Beginner-Intermediate: Dot Versus Crown

Teach notation discipline early so players can reason without losing state.

<Canvas>
  <Story of={StrategyStories.DotVsCrownCycle} />
</Canvas>

### What to look for

- Dot means provisional rejection, not final commitment.
- Crown means committed placement and immediate propagation.
- Good notation reduces repeated scanning.

## Intermediate: Region Elimination

Show how a region can collapse to one legal tile and force a placement.

  <Story of={RegionStories.RegionCandidateCollapse} />

### What to look for

- Count remaining legal tiles inside a region.
- A forced region placement should trigger row and column pruning.
- Re-check nearby regions immediately after propagation.

## Endgame: Candidate Narrowing

When only a few rows or columns remain unresolved, compare candidates directly.

<Canvas>
  <Story of={EndgameStories.TwoCandidateRows} />
</Canvas>

<Canvas>
  <Story of={EndgameStories.FinalCommit} />
</Canvas>

### What to look for

- Identify unresolved rows with only two candidates.
- Use shared columns and touching constraints to eliminate one branch.
- Commit the final legal pair and verify no conflicts remain.

## Suggested Coaching Script

- Ask the learner what changed after each crown.
- Require a short reason before each commitment.
- End each section with one transferable heuristic.
